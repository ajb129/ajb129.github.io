Unit 26
Conjunctions

Conjunctions connect words, phrases, and clause layers. They do so by connecting elements of equivalent grammatical ‘status’. For example, nouns phrases can be coordinated with other noun phrases, clauses can be coordinated with other clauses, but you cannot directly coordinate a noun phrase with a clause.

    With the annotation, all instances of coordination involve the projection(s) of CONJP layers of structure. We can search for this structure by searching for CONJP nodes with pattern (26.1).

(26.1)
/^CONJP\b/

    To find instances of coordination that involve elements of a particular grammatical status, we can place conditions on the elements that come under the CONJP projection. Thus, to find instances of coordination with noun phrase structure, we can use pattern (26.2).

(26.2)
/^CONJP\b/ < /^NP\b/

For example, (26.3) is found with (26.2).

(26.3)
The prize includes flights , accommodation and the cost of a hire car for a week .

    To find instances of coordination with noun phrase structure, we can use pattern (26.4).

(26.4)
/^CONJP\b/ < /^ILYR\b/

For example, (26.5) is found with (26.4).

(26.5)
She went over_to her , and she said , “ What are you drawing ? ”

    To find instances of coordination with adjective phrase structure, we can use the pattern (26.6).

(26.6)
/^CONJP\b/ < /^ADJP\b/

For example, (26.7) is found with (26.6).

(26.7)
He 's plump , but not fat .

    To find instances of coordination with adverb phrase structure, we can use pattern (26.8).

(26.8)
/^CONJP\b/ < (/^ADVP/ !== /ADVP-/)

For example, (26.9) is found with (26.8).

(26.9)
Through reading in particular , pupils have a chance to develop culturally , emotionally , intellectually , socially and spiritually .

    To find instances of coordination with preposition phrase structure, we can use pattern (26.10).

(26.10)
/^CONJP\b/ < /^PP\b/

For example, (26.11) is found with (26.10).

(26.11)
English has a pre-eminent place in education and in society .

26.1   Correlative conjunctions

Correlative conjunctions have two conjuncts and involve an initial introductory conjunction word that is matched with a main conjoining conjunctive word that separates the two conjuncts. We can use the pattern (26.12) that searches for an initial conjunction word to find instances of the construction.

(26.12)
/^CONJ\b/ $.. /^CONJP\b/

For example, (26.13) is found with (26.12).

(26.13)
There were no tracks of either hoofs or boots .

26.2   Connecting clauses with coordinating conjunctions

Clause content can be connected at different levels of clause structure. To see this we can look for different placement of conjunction structure within clause structure. Thus, pattern (26.14) finds conjunction structure that is the only structure immediately below the top level of the matrix clause.

(26.14)
(/^ILYR\b/ < /^CONJP\b/) >: /^IP-MAT/

For example, (26.15) is found with (26.14).

(26.15)

Pattern (26.16) finds conjunction structure that is above the subject, and consequently all of the conjunct need to contain their own subject.

(26.16)
/^ILYR\b/ < (/^ILYR\b/ < /SBJ/) < /^CONJP\b/

For example, (26.17) is found with (26.16).

(26.17)
In the summer holidays I stayed at my cousin 's and we went swimming quite often .

Pattern (26.18) finds conjunction structure that is below the level of the subject, and consequently none of the conjunct are able to contain a subject themselves.

(26.18)
(/^ILYR\b/ < /^CONJP\b/) $ /SBJ/

For example, (26.19) is found with (26.18).

(26.19)
We went home by the Manx Maid and got back to our house in time for tea .

Pattern (26.20) finds conjunction indicated at the level of the discourse.

(26.20)
/^CONJ\b/ > /^IP-MAT/

For example, (26.21) is found with (26.20).

(26.21)
And he collapsed and died instantly .

26.3   Connecting clauses with adverbs

In addition to the coordinating conjunction words and, but, and or, it also possible to connect clauses with adverbs such as so and yet. We can find such adverb use with pattern (26.22).

(26.22)
/^ADVP-CNT/ > /^CONJP\b/

For example, (26.23) is found with (26.22).

(26.23)
It 's dangerous , so you should walk on the sidewalk .

26.4   Reducing clause content with coordination

When conjuncts with similar internal structure are connected, you can often reduce the content in the second conjunct.

Pattern (26.24) finds clausal conjuncts that lack a verb.

(26.24)
/^CONJP\b/ < (/^ILYR\b/ !< /^ILYR\b/ !< /CAT/ !< /^(H|B|D|V)/)

For example, (26.25) is found with (26.24).

(26.25)
London was again in turmoil ; but this time over a question of trade rivalry .
