Unit 14

Negative Yes-No Questions and Tag Questions


14.1    Yes-No questions

You can ask a Yes/No question by giving any of the following words an initial sentence placement:

  • a modal word (MD: can, will, ought)
  • a finite form of HAVE (HVD: had) (HVP: has, have)
  • a finite form of BE (BED: was, were) (BEP: is, am, are)
  • a finite form of DO (DOD: did) (DOP: does, do)

You can see some examples of Yes-No questions in (14.1):

(14.1)
a.
Can you afford food?
b.
Have you got money for school tomorrow?
c.
Is all art ambiguous?
d.
Do you?

14.2    Negative Yes-No questions

You can ask a negative Yes-No question by including the negation word not (NEG) or its contracted variant n't (NEG;_clitic_) within the the Yes-No question you ask.

    The negative Yes-No questions of (14.3) are formed by adding n't to the Yes-No questions of (14.1) above.

(14.3)
a.
Can't you afford food?
b.
Haven't you got money for school tomorrow?
c.
Isn't all art ambiguous?
d.
Don't you?

14.3    Finding examples of negative Yes-No questions

With search pattern (14.5), we can find corpus examples of negative Yes-No questions.

(14.5)

  $ /\bSBJ/=sbj
  $,, (/^(MD|HVD|HVP|BED|BEP|DOD|DOP)\b/=op
         $.. =sbj
         >> (/^IP-SUB/=ip
               > (/^CP-QUE/ [ !> (__ > __) | > /^utterance/ ])
               !<< (/^IP\b/ << =op)

14.4    Tag questions

To find tag questions, you can use pattern (14.11).

(14.11)
/^PRN/ > /^IP-MAT/ < (/^CP-QUE/ < (/^IP-SUB/ <-1 /^NP-SBJ/))

1.
Observe in the hits returned by pattern (14.11) how the subject internal to the highlighted tag question is a pronoun with a form that will match the subject of the matrix clause. Is this always the case?
2.

3.
Do tag questions invite answers or are they used rhetorically? A question is used rhetorically when it is asked with the purpose of making a statement rather than getting an answer.
