Chapter 6

Clause subordination


6.1    Introduction

This chapter details cases of clause subordination.

    When a clause provides the argument content for a containing clause, such as being the direct object, it is a subordinate clause. Rules for clause arguments are discussed in section 6.2 (that-clauses), section 6.3 (to-infinitive clauses) and section 6.4 (embedded questions). Section 6.4 also notes the possibility for embedded questions to serve as the complement component of preposition phrases.

    When a subordinate clause has a subject role within the containing clause, it will often occur as a notional subject extraposed to the right edge of the containing clause and in co-occurrence with a provisional subject it of the containing clause. Section 6.5 notes how parsing captures this co-occurrence as a ripple effect. Section 6.6 shows examples where a derived subject noun phrase co-occurs with an extraposed notional subject that is a to-infinitive clause.

    As a contrast to having embedded to-infinitive clauses that are extraposed notional subjects, section 6.7 looks at the catenative adjective construction, where an embedded to-infinitive clause acts to extend the embedding clause.

    Section 6.8 shows how a clause can be the subordinate component of a subordinate conjunction. Section 6.9 covers the possibility of having a participle clause as the complement of a preposition to give a preposition phrase.

    Section 6.10 details a wide variety of relative clause structures, where a subordinate clause acts as a noun phrase internal modifier with an internal absence of a selected clause item, possibly with an initial phrase containing a relative pronoun.

    The chapter ends with section 6.11 adding support for cleft constructions.


6.2    That-clauses

A that-clause is a finite embedded clause where the initial word can be the complementizer word that tagged C. Annotation allows a that-clause to be identified from the CP-THT label of its topmost layer. This label may be extended with function information through the Ext parameter of the cp_that rules of (6.1).

    [rule 1] of (6.1) will construct a CP-THT layer that has as its only element a finite clause layer (IP-SUB), which in turn contains all the clause content as identified with clause_that_layer of (6.3), including any initial complementizer. Complementizer presence or absence can depend on the Type parameter, through settings:

  • with_c
  • without_c

    If there is conjunction at the highest structural level of a that-clause, then this happens inside the IP-SUB layer within an ILYR (clause internal) layer constructed with [rule 2] of (6.1), which calls clause_that_layer to identify the first conjunct and recursive cp_that_tail of (6.2) to identify all non-initial conjuncts.

(6.1)

    atom_concat('CP-⁠THT',Ext,Label)

  },

  cp_that_tail(Type,Store,CL,[]).

    Recursive cp_that_tail of (6.2) identifies each non-initial conjunct through a call of clause_that_layer. Conjunct collection terminates with [rule 1] when there is a conjunction word followed by content for a final conjunct. Non-final conjuncts are identified with [rule 2] and must occur immediately after non_final punctuation.

6.3    To-infinitive clauses

A to-infinitive clause is a non-finite clause with the word to tagged TO and a following verb in the infinitive form. In addition to being able to extend the same clause as part of a catenative construction (with topmost layer labelled IP-INF-CAT; as seen in section 5.4.2), a to-infinitive clause can also form a distinct clause that is an argument for a containing clause. Examples where a to-infinitive clause is an object argument (with topmost layer labelled IP-INF-OB1) are seen in sections 4.4.7 (with code ;~Tt), 4.4.8 (with code ;~Tnt), 4.5.5 (with code ;~Dn.t) and 4.6.1 (with code ;~Cn.a, where the to-infinitive co-occurs as a notional object (IP-INF-NOB1) with a provisional object it).

    The rules of to_inf_layer in (6.8) identify to-infinitive clauses. The first three rules are non-recursive and pick up from the word list an initial to word. These three rules differ as follows:

  • [rule 1] for an active clause, calls verb_phrase_layer with the clause as infinitive and active
  • [rule 2] for a passive clause, adds a zero logical subject (node('NP-LGS',[node('*',[])])) to the collected parse information and calls verb_phrase_layer with the clause as infinitive and lgs_passive
  • [rule 3] for a lgs_passive clause, that is a passive clause where a logical subject has already occured, calls verb_phrase_layer with the clause as infinitive and lgs_passive

    There are two rules to enable introduction of a clause initial connective particle (which is always for) followed by content for a subject element from which the SbjType replaces an inherited unfilled_sbj value. These two rules differ as follows:

  • [rule 4] for an active clause, marks the subject element as being the grammatical subject (-SBJ)
  • [rule 5] for a passive clause, marks the subject element as being the logical subject (-LGS) and sets the clause type parameter to lgs_passive

6.4    Embedded question clauses

As an initial clause component, an embedded question clause can have:

  • an embedded question clause marker (whether or if) tagged WQ, creating a yes/no question
  • a phrase with interrogative type information, creating a constituent question

Internally, an embedded question clause can be:

  • a finite clause with statement word order (IP-SUB)
  • a to-infinitive clause (IP-INF)

    The topmost layer of an embedded question clause has the CP-QUE label, together with any extension from the Ext parameter taken by cp_embedded_que of (6.10). A cp_embedded_que call can involve a subsequent call of clause_embedded_que_finite_top_layer to identify under an immediately embedded IP-SUB layer content for:

  • [rule 1] a finite clause with statement word order
  • [rule 2] the first conjunct of a conjunction that is a finite clause with statement word order followed by a recursive call to cp_embedded_que_finite_tail to pick up subsequent conjuncts that are finite clauses with statement word order

A cp_embedded_que call can also involve a subsequent call of clause_embedded_que_to_inf_top_layer to identify under an immediately embedded IP-INF layer content for:

  • [rule 3] a to-infinitive clause
  • [rule 4] the first conjunct of a conjunction of to-infinitive clauses with a recursive call to cp_embedded_que_to_inf_tail to pick up subsequent conjuncts

6.4.1    Embedded question clauses that are finite clauses with statement word order

The clause_embedded_que_finite_top_layer rules of (6.14) collect the content of an embedded question clause that is a finite clause with statement word order. This gives rules that differ in how they introduce the embedded question:

  • [rule 1] picks up with comp_wq an initial embedded question clause marker before finding the remaining clause content with clause_top_layer, which receives statement_order as the value for its clause Type parameter.
  • [rule 2] picks up a stored noun phrase with interrogative type information which receives an index coindexed with an *ICH* (interpret constituent here marker) that goes into the Store parameter passed to clause_embedded_que_finite_lower_layer of (6.15), which finds the remaining clause content
  • [rule 3] picks up an adverb phrase with interrogative type information which receives an index coindexed with an *ICH* (interpret constituent here marker) that goes into the Store parameter passed to clause_top_layer, which receives statement_order as the value for its clause Type parameter to find the the remaining clause content
  • [rule 4] picks up an adverb phrase with interrogative type information before finding the the remaining clause content with clause_top_layer, which receives statement_order as the value for its clause Type parameter

    6.8    Subordinate conjunction

    This section gives rules for integrating the subordinate clause component of subordinate conjunctions.     • initial_adverbial from section 2.10
    • adverbial from section 2.10

    6.10.5    Relative clauses with resumptive pronouns

    So far we have seen heads of host noun phrases integrated internally to relative clauses with either a relative pronoun, a relative adverb, a zero trace element or through reduction of the relative clause to a participle clause. Let's call these the standard methods.

        Yet another method is to use a resumptive pronoun. This can be a fall back for when relative clause content becomes too complex internally for the standard methods to work. Integration happens with pronoun(s) picking up the external head as antecedent. As an example, (6.111) has the zero word w('P-ROLE','*') in its word list, so that the relative pronoun will open its content to '*', which we need to assume is the discourse context.

    (6.111)
    | ?-⁠ tphrase_set_string([w('EX','There'), w('BEP',';~ex_V','is'), w('D','a'), w('N','machine;<MACHINE>'), w('P-⁠ROLE','*'), w('RPRO','which'), w('P-⁠CONN','in_order'), w('TO','to'), w('VB',';~Tn','reboot'), w('PRO','it;<MACHINE>'), w('PRO','you'), w('HVP',';~cat_Vt','have'), w('TO','to'), w('VB',';~Tn','stop'), w('PRO','it;<MACHINE>'), w('PUNC','.')]), parse(sentence).

    6.11    Cleft constructions

    The extra verb_complements_by_code rules of (6.113) allow coverage of cleft constructions.

    (6.113)
    verb_complements_by_code(';~cleft_Vn',store([],[]),provisional_sbj,active,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
      noun_phrase('-⁠FOC',non_privileged,L,[node('IP-⁠CLF',IL)|L0]),

      {
        append(Left,Right,[np(ICH)])
      },
      relative_clause_finite_top_layer(IL,[]).

        The rules of (6.113) require the verb code ;~cleft_Vn, selecting as complements:

    • a noun phrase as the focus (-FOC)
    • a cleft clause (IP-CLF) that has the internal structure of a finite relative clause.

        Both rules of (6.113) require the clause SbjType parameter to be set to the provisional_sbj value. This setting is possible with [rule 2] of subject in section 2.5 picking up a word with word class PRO;_provisional_, which can only be an instance of it.
