Chapter 7

Closing remarks

We have now reached the end of the presentation of Definite Clause Grammar (DCG) definitions in this book. When taken together, they provide a system for parsing contemporary English with a wide coverage. However, keeping phrase structure rules straightforward and reducing structural ambiguity has placed limits on direct coverage:

  • Phrase heads need to always be present in the word list. A work around is to have zero heads explicitly represented in the word list, e.g. w('P-CONN','_*_').
  • There is no mechanism to capture extrapositioning that happens with rightward displacements of relative clauses, that-clauses or to-infinitive clauses.
  • There is no support for parentheticals.
  • The support for punctuation is limited.

In regard to limitations on punctuation:

  • There is no support for punctuation internal to phrase layers.
  • Commas have to occur between conjuncts, except between the penultimate and last conjunct which must have a represented conjunction word (CONJ) instead.
  • Commas can occur optionally after adverbials with a clause initial placement or before adverbials with a clause final placement when attached at the highest layer of clause structure.
  • A declarative or imperative sentence requires a full stop or exclamation mark (final punctuation) with a final position.
  • An interrogative sentence requires a question mark (final_question punctuation) with a final position.
