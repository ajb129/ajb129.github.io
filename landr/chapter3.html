Chapter 3

Phrase layers


3.1    Introduction

This chapter considers phrase layers. These are intermediate levels of organisation between a word and the clause. Each phrase is made up of a head word and any dependent(s). The head word is the word that the phrase requires in order to exist and may be the only element of the phrase. A phrase name reflects the word class of the head word. There are four phrase types:

  • noun phrases — with a noun head word (section 3.2)
  • adverb phrases — with an adverb head word (section 3.3)
  • adjective phrases — with an adjective head word (section 3.4)
  • preposition phrases — with a preposition head word (section 3.5)

3.2    Noun phrases

The noun_phrase rule of (3.1) identifies noun phrases by calling noun_phrase_top for list content NL of daughter elements for a node structure with its Label consisting of 'NP' and any extension from the Ext parameter. A contentful Ext gives role/function/indexing information. The identified structure adds to list L, which holds content from outside the noun phrase.

(3.1)
noun_phrase(Ext,Type,[node(Label,NL)|L],L) -⁠-⁠>
  {
    atom_concat('NP',Ext,Label)
  },
  noun_phrase_top(Type,NL,[]).

    Additional to the Ext parameter, there is a Type parameter for a value that goes through to the noun_phrase_top call. There are four possible values, already seen in sections 2.2, 2.3 and 2.4:

  • non_privileged
  • established
  • interrogative
  • relative

Ultimately, this has to be type information expected by internal elements:

  • head nouns that can have no modifiers (see section 2.2)
  • determiners (see section 2.3)
  • genitive pronouns (see section 2.4)
  • an initial adjective phrase with interrogative as its own Type setting, compatible with receiving a Type setting that is interrogative or non_privileged
  • a preposition phrase that receives relative as the Type setting

    This type information will determine how noun phrases can be used as reflected by distribution. This was hinted at already with the question at the end of section 2.2 and, for example, leads to the contrast seen between the successes of (3.2) and (3.3) and the failure of (3.4).

(3.2)
| ?-⁠ tphrase_set_string([w('PRO','He'), w('VBD',';~Tn','fires'), w('PRO','it'), w('PUNC','.')]), parse(sentence).

(IP-⁠MAT
  (NP-⁠SBJ (PRO He))
  (VBD;~Tn fires)
  (NP-⁠OB1 (PRO it))
  (PUNC .))

yes
(3.3)
| ?-⁠ tphrase_set_string([w('PRO','It'), w('VBD',';~Tn','fires'), w('PNX','itself'), w('PUNC','.')]), parse(sentence).

(IP-⁠MAT

3.2.1    The noun phrase top rule

A call of the noun_phrase_top rule (3.5) identifies content for a noun phrase with:

  • [rule 1] a call of noun_head_full (from section 2.2) which will identify a word that essentially contributes a full noun phrase by itself
  • [rule 2] a call of noun_phrase_initial_layer (from section 3.2.2 below)
(3.5)
noun_phrase_top(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun_head_full(Type,L,L0).
noun_phrase_top(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L0).

    Query (3.6) asks if a word with WPRO word class (an interrogative pronoun) is sufficient content for a noun phrase with non_privileged as its Type setting.

(3.6)
| ?-⁠ tphrase_set_string([w('WPRO','who')]), parse(noun_phrase('',non_privileged)).

(NP (WPRO who))

yes

The affirmative response and tree output of (3.6) follows from the call structure of (3.7).

(3.7)
noun_phrase non_privileged
noun_phrase_top non_privileged
noun_head_full non_privileged
[w('WPRO','who')]

    Note how non_privileged as the noun phrase Type setting passes down the call structure of (3.7) to determine what can be appropriate content. In contrast to (3.6), query (3.8) fails: The noun phrase call provides non_privileged type information, but the word list contains a word with RPRO word class (a relative pronoun) requiring relative as the Type setting.

(3.8)
| ?-⁠ tphrase_set_string([w('RPRO','who')]), parse(noun_phrase('',non_privileged)).

no

3.2.2    Initial noun phrase layers

With (3.6), we have seen the simplest form for a noun phrase, which involves a single word that contributes content for noun_head_full. This section delves slightly deeper into possible noun phrase structure by considering what a noun_phrase_initial_layer call of (3.9) may return.

    Among other things, a noun_phrase_initial_layer call opens the possibility of having coordinate structures at the top level of the noun phrase. With (3.9), there are two ways to achieve this highest level of noun phrase coordination:

  • [rule 5] with structure involving an initial conjunct word followed by the content of a first conjunct followed by another conjunct word followed by the content of a second conjunct
  • [rule 6] with a call of noun_phrase that collects content for a first conjunct followed by a call to noun_phrase_initial_tail of (3.10) that begins a recursive accumulation of the content of further conjuncts
(3.9)
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  determiner_layer(Type,L,L1),
  internal_np_higher_layer(L1,L0).
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  {
    member(Type,[non_privileged,interrogative])
  },
  adjective_phrase('',store([],[]),interrogative,L,L1),
  internal_np_higher_layer(L1,L0).
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  {
    member(Type,[non_privileged,established])
  },
  noun_head(L,L0).
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  {
    member(Type,[non_privileged,established])
  },
  internal_np_higher_layer(L,L0).
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,[node('NLYR',[CONJ1,node('NLYR',NL1),node('CONJP',[CONJ2,node('NLYR',NL2)])])|L],L) -⁠-⁠>
  conj(CONJ1),
  noun_phrase_top(Type,NL1,[]),
  conj(CONJ2),
  noun_phrase_top(Type,NL2,[]).
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,[node('NLYR',[node('NLYR',NL)|CL])|L],L) -⁠-⁠>
  noun_phrase_top(Type,NL,[]),
  noun_phrase_initial_tail(Type,CL,[]).
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L1),
  preposition_phrase('',established,L1,L0).
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  {
    Type == relative
  },
  noun_phrase_initial_layer(established,L,L1),
  preposition_phrase('',relative,L1,L0).
noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun_phrase_initial_layer(Type,L,L1),
  relative_clause(L1,L0).
(3.10)
noun_phrase_initial_tail(Type,[node('CONJP',[CONJ,node('NLYR',NL)])|L],L) -⁠-⁠>
  conj(CONJ),
  noun_phrase_top(Type,NL,[]).
noun_phrase_initial_tail(Type,[PU,node('CONJP',[node('NLYR',NL)])|L],L0) -⁠-⁠>
  punc(non_final,[PU],[]),
  noun_phrase_top(Type,NL,[]),
  noun_phrase_initial_tail(Type,L,L0).

    The parse of (3.11) illustrates a noun phrase with a top level coordination. The resulting noun phrase structure has an NLYR (noun phrase internal layer) as its only daughter element. The NLYR itself has three conjuncts, each of which falls under its own NLYR node.

(3.11)
| ?-⁠ tphrase_set_string([w('PRO','you'), w('PUNC',','), w('PRO','he'), w('CONJ','and'), w('PRO','I')]), parse(noun_phrase('-⁠SBJ',established)).

    (NLYR (PRO you))

      (NLYR (PRO he)))

      (NLYR (PRO I)))))

yes

This gives call structure that branches to the right, while leading to the return of conjuncts placed immediately under the same NLYR of the output tree in (3.11).

3.2.3    Internal noun phrase layers

Recursive noun_phrase_initial_layer calls of (3.9) can establish noun phrase coordination or add post-head modifiers at the highest levels of noun phrase structure. A non-recursive call is preparation for delving deeper into noun phrase structure by reaching internal_np_higher_layer calls that, through the non-recursive (3.17), reach internal_np_lower_layer calls, to terminate identifying structure with the non-recursive rules of (3.20).

(3.17)
internal_np_higher_layer(L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  internal_np_lower_layer(_,L,L0).

    There are also recursive internal_np_higher_layer rules in (3.18) that allow for:

  • [rule 1] adjective phrases with established type information as pre-head modifiers
  • [rule 2] single word present participle (-ing) clauses as pre-head modifiers
  • [rule 3] single word passive voice past participle (-ed/-en) clauses as pre-head modifiers
  • [rule 4] preposition phrases as post-head modifiers
  • [rule 5] relative clauses as post-head modifiers
  • [rule 6] the integration of coordination
(3.18)
internal_np_higher_layer(L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  adjective_phrase('',store([],[]),established,L,L1),
  internal_np_higher_layer(L1,L0).
internal_np_higher_layer([node('IP-⁠PPL',[node('VAG;~I',[node(Word,[])])])|L],L0) -⁠-⁠>
  [w('VAG',';~I',Word)],
  internal_np_higher_layer(L,L0).
internal_np_higher_layer([node('IP-⁠PPL',[node('NP-⁠LGS',[node('*',[])]),node('VVN;~Tn',[node(Word,[])])])|L],L0) -⁠-⁠>
  [w('VVN',';~Tn',Word)],
  internal_np_higher_layer(L,L0).
internal_np_higher_layer(L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  internal_np_higher_layer(L,L1),
  preposition_phrase('',established,L1,L0).
internal_np_higher_layer(L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  internal_np_higher_layer(L,L1),
  relative_clause(L1,L0).
internal_np_higher_layer([node('NLYR',[node('NLYR',NL)|CL])|L],L) -⁠-⁠>
  internal_np_higher_layer(NL,[]),
  internal_np_higher_tail(CL,[]).

    For coordinations of internal_np_higher_layer structure, conjuncts subsequent to the first are accumulated with internal_np_higher_tail of (3.19).

(3.19)
internal_np_higher_tail([node('CONJP',[CONJ,node('NLYR',NL)])|L],L) -⁠-⁠>
  conj(CONJ),
  internal_np_higher_layer(NL,[]).
internal_np_higher_tail([PU,node('CONJP',[node('NLYR',NL)])|L],L0) -⁠-⁠>
  punc(non_final,[PU],[]),
  internal_np_higher_layer(NL,[]),
  internal_np_higher_tail(L,L0).

    Non-recursive internal_np_lower_tail calls of (3.20) involve finding a noun that will be the head noun, either:

  • [rule 1] without a complement, for a simple type setting
  • [rules 2–4] with a complement, for a complex type setting

A complement can be:

  • [rule 2] a to-infinitive clause found with ip_to_inf of section 6.5.2
  • [rule 3] a finite that-clause with an overt instance of the complementiser word that found with cp_that of section 6.2 called with its Type set to with_c
  • [rule 4] an embedded question clause found with cp_embedded_que of section 6.4
(3.20)
internal_np_lower_layer(simple,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun(L,L0).
internal_np_lower_layer(complex,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun(L,L1),
  ip_to_inf('',store([],[]),L1,L0).
internal_np_lower_layer(complex,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun(L,L1),
  cp_that('',with_c,store([],[]),L1,L0).
internal_np_lower_layer(complex,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun(L,L1),
  cp_embedded_que('',store([],[]),L1,L0).

    There are also recursive internal_np_lower_layer rules in (3.21) to integrate noun modifiers that are:

  • [rule 1] a noun
  • [rule 2] simple type internal_np_lower_layer structure
  • [rule 3] simple type conjoined internal_np_lower_layer structure

There is also:

  • [rule 4] coordination of internal_np_lower_layer structure
(3.21)
internal_np_lower_layer(Type,L,L0) -⁠-⁠>
  noun(L,L1),
  internal_np_lower_layer(Type,L1,L0).
internal_np_lower_layer(Type,[node('NLYR',[NL1,NL2|NL3])|L],L0) -⁠-⁠>
  internal_np_lower_layer(simple,[NL1,NL2|NL3],[]),
  internal_np_lower_layer(Type,L,L0).
internal_np_lower_layer(Type,[node('NLYR',[node('NLYR',[node('NLYR',NL)|CL])])|L],L0) -⁠-⁠>
  internal_np_lower_layer(simple,NL,[]),
  internal_np_lower_tail(simple,CL,[]),
  internal_np_lower_layer(Type,L,L0).
internal_np_lower_layer(Type,[node('NLYR',[node('NLYR',NL)|CL])|L],L) -⁠-⁠>
  internal_np_lower_layer(Type,NL,[]),
  internal_np_lower_tail(Type,CL,[]).

    For coordinations of internal_np_lower_layer structure, conjuncts subsequent to the first are accumulated with internal_np_lower_tail of (3.22).

(3.22)
internal_np_lower_tail(Type,[node('CONJP',[CONJ,node('NLYR',NL)])|L],L) -⁠-⁠>
  conj(CONJ),
  internal_np_lower_layer(Type,NL,[]).
internal_np_lower_tail([PU,node('CONJP',[node('NLYR',NL)])|L],L0) -⁠-⁠>
  punc(non_final,[PU],[]),
  internal_np_lower_layer(Type,NL,[]),
  internal_np_lower_tail(Type,L,L0).

    In (3.23) we can see a demonstration of how a word list containing only a single common noun word gives content for a resulting noun phrase.

3.2.5    Determiner layer

The call structures revealed by (3.24) and (3.26) above allow for elements around the noun words of a noun phrase. Notably, a call to noun_phrase_initial_layer of (3.9) allows for placement of a determiner layer before the noun phrase head word. The determiner_layer rules of (3.27) identify determiner layers which are sensitive to the type information carried by the calling noun phrase.

(3.27)

  det(Type,L,L0).

  noun_phrase_genm_layer(Type,NL,[]).

    The word list of (3.28) contains a determiner word, followed by a common noun word that can serve as a noun phrase head, followed by a coordination word, and ending with a second common noun word that can also serve as a noun phrase head. 3.4    Adjective phrases

The two adjective_phrase rules of (3.41) differ in their Type setting. For [rule 1], Type parameter settings are:

  • established
  • interrogative
  • relative
  • the value from a subject type setting, so: filled_sbj, there_sbj, provisional_sbj or derived_sbj

    For [rule 2], the Type setting is:

  • non_privileged

The non_privileged setting of [rule 2] has the consequence that the adjective phrase is built in accordance to having established or interrogative type information.

(3.41)
adjective_phrase(Ext,Store,Type,[node(Label,AL)|L],L) -⁠-⁠>

    Type1 == non_privileged,
    member(Type2,[established,interrogative]),
    atom_concat('ADJP',Ext,Label)
  },
  adjective_phrase_layer(store([],[]),Type2,AL,[]).

The rules of (3.41) will identify an adjective phrase by calling adjective_phrase_layer of (3.42) to accumulate list content (AL) to place under an ADJP labelled structure, possibly with a label extension as role/function/indexing information. There is an Ext parameter to provide any label extension. There is also a Store parameter, which, for catenative adjective phrases, can pass on index information from the containing clause for identified clause elements that await integration into the clause.

    The non-recursive rules of (3.42) [rules 1–3], establish the head word as a word found with a call of adj (see section 2.7):

  • [rule 1], when the Type is interrogative or relative, this information passes on to calls of adverb_phrase (see [rule 6] of (3.42)) whereupon the Type information for the rest of the adjective phrase becomes established
  • [rule 2], with a Type setting that is a subject type setting (so: filled_sbj, there_sbj, provisional_sbj or derived_sbj), brings the additional need for word list content that will support a call of to_inf_layer (examples are in sections 4.2.1 and 6.7)
  • [rule 3], with a Type setting of established, requires a word list that contains only an adjective word
  • [rule 4], with a Type setting of established, brings the additional need for word list content that will support a call of cp_that called with its Type set to with_c (see section 6.2)

    Calls to adjective_phrase_layer of (3.42) can also be recursive, allowing, when the Type setting is established, the parse to:

  • [rule 7] pick up pre-head modifier adverb phrases
  • [rule 8] pick up post-head modifier preposition phrases
