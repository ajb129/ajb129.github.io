Chapter 5

Clause layers


5.1    Introduction

This chapter introduces clause (IP) layers. Section 5.2 has rules for building clause structure. Section 5.3 is concerned with matrix interrogative clauses where the subject is not under question. Section 5.4 sees an expansion of potential for clause structure with catenative verbs. Section 5.5 adds rules to join up clause content for statement word order. Section 5.6 looks at ripple effects in the clause caused by existential there.


5.2    Clause structure

The opening elements of a clause are established with the clause_top_layer rules of (5.1) and (5.2) below. These rules have a clause type parameter, which is set to either:

  • statement_order
  • imperative_clause
  • tag_question
  • matrix_interrogative
  • matrix_constituent_interrogative

The rules also have a Displaced parameter, which takes a list value that accumulates indexing information for dispaced items.

    The ten rules of (5.1) are all non-recursive. They each have something to say about the clause subject. For rule 2, with the clause Type parameter set to imperative_clause, there can be no subject. The other rules need to find either:

  • [rules 1 and 3–8] noun phrase or subject indicating content found with a call of subject (from section 2.5)
  • [rules 9 and 10] the subject is inherited as an np(ICH) displaced item for a clause whose Type parameter is matrix_constituent_interrogative and the SbjType parameter is set to either filled_subject (rule 9) or derived_subject (rule 10)

Rules finding subject content with a call of subject vary in regards to where the subject occurs in the word list and the value taken by the clause Type parameter, as follows:

  • [rule 1] the subject call finds its content as an initial portion of the word list and sets the SbjType parameter for a clause whose Type parameter is statement_order
  • [rules 3–5] the subject call with SbjType parameter limited to either filled_subject or expletive_subject finds its content with final position in the word list for a clause whose Type parameter is tag_question
  • [rules 6–8] the subject call: (i) finds its content as a portion of the word list that is immediately after components (see section 5.3) identified with either do_operator_layer (rule 6), operator_layer (rule 7) or have_be_or_md_cat_finite_layer (rule 8), and (ii) sets the SbjType parameter for a clause whose Type parameter is matrix_interrogative

    There are also the eleven recursive rules of (5.2) to enable:

  • [rule 1] a clause initial adverbial, possibly followed by non_final punctuation
  • [rule 2] a clause final adverbial, possibly preceded by non_final punctuation
  • [rule 3] a clause initial noun phrase with type interrogative that is a displaced item, changing the clause type information from matrix_interrogative into matrix_constituent_interrogative
  • [rule 4] a clause initial preposition phrase with type interrogative that is a displaced item, changing the clause type information from matrix_interrogative into matrix_constituent_interrogative
  • [rule 5] a clause initial adjective_phrase phrase with type interrogative that is a displaced item, changing the clause type information from matrix_interrogative into matrix_constituent_interrogative
  • [rule 6] a clause initial adverb phrase with type interrogative that is a displaced item, changing the clause type information from matrix_interrogative into matrix_constituent_interrogative
  • [rule 7] a clause initial adverb phrase with type interrogative that has a -NIM (unselected adverbial) function, changing the clause type information from matrix_interrogative into matrix_constituent_interrogative
  • [rule 8] a clause initial noun phrase with type general that is a displaced item for a statement_order clause
  • [rule 9] a clause initial preposition phrase with type general that is a displaced item for a statement_order clause
  • [rule 10] a clause initial utterance phrase that is a displaced item, offset by quotation punctuation and possibly followed by non_final punctuation, for a statement_order clause
  • [rule 11] conjuncts of coordinate structure, with non-initial conjuncts gathered by calls to clause_top_tail of (5.3)

    Note how with the rules of (5.2): noun phrases [rules 3 and 8], preposition phrases [rule 4 and 9], adjective phrases [rule 5], adverb phrases [rules 6 and 7], and utterances [rule 10], all of which are parsed as displaced elements, receive an index coindexed with an *ICH* (interpret constituent here marker) that is added to the Displaced parameter to ultimately be inherited as a selected item for a verb_complements_top_layer call from chapter 4.

    Also note how rules 1 and 2 of (5.2) have adverbial calls (from section 2.10) to pick up:

  • discourse coordination words (e.g., And, But)
  • clause level interjection (e.g., aah, eh, ummmmm)
  • reaction signals (e.g., good_grief, really, yes, wow)
  • formulaic expressions (e.g., good_afternoon, you_see, thank_you)
  • adverb phrases with -NIM (unselected adverbial) function
  • preposition phrases with -NIM (unselected adverbial) function
  • the subordinate component of a subordinate conjunction

    Rule 2 for clause_top_layer of (5.1) calls verb_phrase_layer of (5.4). This subsequent rule layer is needed to ensure there is a place for introducing inflection and voice information before the verb and its complements are encountered. Having the clause type value imperative_clause, rule 2 of (5.1) gives the values of imperative_inflection for the Infl (inflection) parameter and active_voice for the Voice parameter.

    The full range of options for the Infl parameter are:

  • finite_inflection
  • imperative_inflection
  • infinitive_inflection
  • past_participle
  • present_participle

Options for the Voice parameter are:

  • active_voice
  • passive_voice

    From the rules of (5.4), in addition to passing on information for verb_complements_top_layer [rule 1], there is, provided the clause has imperative inflection, a non-recursive rule [rule 2] to support the presence of an imperative DO with operator function (so tagged DO and with no verb code) possibly followed by a clitic negation (added with optional_clitic_negation from section 2.10).

    Calls to verb_phrase_layer can also be recursive, allowing the parse to either:

  • [rule 3] pick up an initial negation with a call to neg (from section 2.10)
  • [rule 4] pick up initial adverb phrases with a call to adverb_phrase (from section 3.3)
  • [rule 5] pick up clause final adverbials with a call to adverbial (from section 6.6)
  • [rule 6] start a conjunction structure with non-initial conjuncts gathered by calls to verb_phrase_tail of (5.5)

5.3    Matrix interrogatives

This section considers matrix interrogative clauses where the subject is not under question. Prior to its subject, such a clause will have either a finite DO, a modal, a finite HAVE, a finite BE, or a modal catenative, any of which can be followed by the contracted form of negation (n't). This section will add rules to match these pre-subject components, including the option for negation, so that they can be integrated into a parse with calls of clause_top_layer from section 5.2 above.


5.3.1    DO operator layer

Having the do_operator_layer rule of (5.8) leads to inclusion of a finite DO and also allows for an optional presence for negation as clitic n't (integrated with optional_clitic_negation from section 2.10).

(5.8)
do_operator_layer([node(Tag,[node(Word,[])])|L],L0) -->
  [w(Tag,'',Word)],
  {
    member(Tag,['DOP','DOD'])
  },
  optional_clitic_negation(L,L0).

    As example content for a constituent question that needs an operator_layer call to parse a finite DO, consider (5.9).

(5.9)
| ?- tphrase_set_string([w('WPRO','What'), w('DOP','','do'), w('PRO','you'), w('VB',';~Tn','need')]), parse(clause_top_layer(matrix_interrogative,[])).

  (DOP do)
  (NP-SBJ (PRO you))

  (NP-OB1 *ICH*-133))

yes

Having the two have_be_or_md_cat_finite_layer rules of (5.14) allows for the inclusion of either a finite HAVE word [rule 1], a finite BE word [rule 2], or a modal catenative word [rule 3]. All three rules allow for an optional presence for negation as clitic n't.

5.4    Clause structure with a main verb and prior catenative verbs

So far we have seen clause structure with only a single verb, but a clause might include a sequence of verbs. The main verb is either the only verb of a clause or the last verb to occur in a sequence of verbs of the same clause. Verbs of a verb sequence that are prior to the main verb are called catenative verbs.

    Section 4.10 provides verb_complements_by_code rules for the full range of catenative verbs. Now we focus on resulting clause structure from catenative verbs with code:

  • ;~cat_Vt selecting a to-infinitive clause complement (section 5.4.1)
  • ;~cat_Ve selecting an active past participle (-ed/-en) clause complement (section 5.4.2)
  • ;~cat_Vg selecting an active present participle (-ing) clause complement (section 5.4.3)
  • ;~cat_Ve_passive_ selecting a passive past participle (-ed/-en) clause complement (section 5.4.4)

5.4.1    Catenative verbs with to-infinitive complements
