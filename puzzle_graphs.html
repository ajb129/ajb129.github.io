About English Puzzle Graphs

Alastair Butler

Hirosaki University




1   Aims

You can study English while having fun with English puzzle graphs. You will learn about word interactions while solving puzzles. The puzzles will help you understand how word connections can guide the building of sentences. Goals for working through the puzzles are:

  • to learn about the components that go into building up a sentence
  • to get a stronger feeling for how words interact with each other (word dependencies)
  • to learn how to structure content for complex sentences, which are sentences with multiple clauses
  • to see how sentences can connect with other sentences

    Each puzzle takes the form of a graph of words with labelled arrows that connect the graph nodes. The labelled arrows are clues for building either a sentence fragment, a single sentence or multiple sentences of English. The puzzle task is to turn the graph of words with connecting clues into sentences, called ‘solution sentences’. You can check your success by seeing if your solution sentence(s) match the suggested answer solution sentence(s).


2   How to read a puzzle graph

In a puzzle graph, word content for solution sentences is found outside of square brackets (‘[’, ‘]’) and is always accompanied by numbering inside round brackets (‘(’, ‘)’) to say the word position within the solution sentence(s). Word position numbering involves two numbers separated by an underscore character (‘_’). The first number says which solution sentence the word belongs to. The second number says the position of the word within its solution sentence.

    Square bracket content tells us about how words function, that is, what words do in their sentences. These are important clues that help to say where words should go to form solution sentences.

    Words with position numbering and/or square bracket function information can provide the content for a graph node. They can also provide the label for a directed arrow that connects graph nodes. An arrow connects content from one node (the governor node) to another node (the dependent node). It is typically square bracket function information that is used to label a directed arrow.

    It is possible to ‘reverse’ a node connection by adding [of] to the end of the arrow label, e.g., [arg0] changes to [arg0][of]. This has the consequence that what would have been the governor node is made the dependent node and what would have been the dependent node is made the governor node. Use of a reverse connection is illustrated below in section 3.3.

    Square bracket content should not be included in your completed solution sentence(s). In contrast, all words that have position information should be included in your completed solution sentence(s). You never have to modify the forms of words, so a word should be entered into its solution sentence as you find it in the graph.

    Most of the words you need for the solution sentence(s) of a puzzle are found inside its graph, but sometimes you will need to add additional ‘invisible’ words. Some of the invisible words will turn out to be words that are involved with referencing what is talked about. These words can be:

  • indefinites (e.g., a, an, some) used to introduce new items (graph nodes) for discussion
  • definites (e.g., the, these, this) used to reference unique new items (graph nodes) for discussion
  • bound pronouns (e.g., he, her, themselves) that reference already established graph nodes introduced because of earlier words
  • definites with following nouns (e.g., the garden, the tool-shed) when used to reference already familiar graph nodes introduced because of earlier words
  • proper nouns (e.g., Peter, Mr. McGregor) when used to reference already familiar graph nodes introduced because of earlier instances of the same proper noun

Note that unbound pronouns do have graph presence.

    Other invisible words are words that contribute no contentful information, but rather have a formal role of marking grammatical structure:

  • relative pronouns used at the start of a relative clause, e.g., who, which, that (Mr. McGregor came up with a sieve, which he intended to pop upon the top of Peter)
  • the complementizer that used at the start of an embedded clause (Mr. McGregor was sure that Peter was somewhere, I am sorry to say that Peter was not very well)
  • the infinitive marker to used at the start of a to-infinitive clause (a beautiful thing to hide in, Peter sat down to rest )
  • existential there used to signal a there construction (there were four little Rabbits)
  • dummy it, e.g., in extraposition constructions (Peter thought it best to go away), and cleft constructions (It was the second jacket that Peter had lost)

    It is also possible for graph content to involve the ‘#’ symbol. This marks a word absence that has an abstract location in its solution sentence, as illustrated below in section 3.2.


3   Tips for reaching solution sentences

This section offers tips for reaching solution sentences. This starts with a simple puzzle. Then we gradually pick up skills to tackle more complex puzzles.

3.1   A simple puzzle

To form a solution sentence you can start by looking into a puzzle graph for an arrow that includes [arg0] in its label. The governor node for such an arrow will be the verb content for the clause, possibly with other supporting words, while the dependent node will be the head word corresponding to the subject, that is, the ‘do-er’ or ‘be-er’ or ‘have-er’ of the clause.

    Consider figure 1. This gives the puzzle graph for a single clause. By finding the arrow labelled [arg0], we identify were(1_6) as the main verb. We also identify Rabbits(1_9) as the ‘be-er’ (subject).

Figure 1: Puzzle graph for a single clause

    When the main verb of a clause is a form of the verb BE like were and there is no subject predicative connection ([prd2], as in Figure 2 below) or closely related ([clr]) connection, then the [arg0] dependent will typically need to occur after were. Moreover, were should occur after an existential there. With this knowledge, we can construct (1).

(1)
there were Rabbits

    With (1), we already have a full sentence of English! However, (1) is not a complete solution sentence for figure 1, since there are words that have yet to be used. Two of these words come at the ends of arrows that have Rabbits(1_9) as their governor node. These words act to modify Rabbits(1_9). Specifically, there is a numeral modifier to tell us about the number of rabbits (four(1_7)), and there is an attributive modifier to tell us about the size of the rabbits (little(1_8)). In English, a numeral modifier should occur before an attributive adjective modifier to give (2).

(2)
there were four little Rabbits

    Figure 1 also contains content for a temporal modifier with an arrow labelled [modifier][tmp] that has the main verb as its governor node and that points to the adverb Once(1_1). This adverb has itself a modifier labelled with the preposition word upon(1_2) and with time(1_4) as the dependent word. After connecting these words, we end up with the solution sentence of (3).

(3)
Once upon a time there were four little Rabbits

    Note that (3) includes a as the third word, and yet this word is not part of figure 1. This is an example of an ‘invisible’ word that, as already noted in section 2, we might sometimes need to add in addition to the words from the puzzle graph. We can gain confirmation that this extra word insertion is needed from observing that the resulting solution sentence now has words with positions that follow the word numbering of figure 1, as (4) makes explicit.

(4)
Once(1_1) upon(1_2) a(1_3) time(1_4) there(1_5) were(1_6) four(1_7) little(1_8) Rabbits(1_9)

    As another example, consider figure 2, which also has content for a single clause sentence.

Figure 2: Puzzle graph for another single clause

Searching for an [arg0] labelled arrow, we find that were is once again the main verb of the clause, now as were(2_3). The ‘be-er’ (subject) of the clause is names(2_2), which itself has an arrow labelled [genv] (genitive), which takes us to the genitive pronoun their(2_1). There is also an arrow labelled [prd2] (subject predicative), which provides content for ‘what their names were’ by taking us to and(2_7), which in turn takes us to four conjuncts. Each conjunct is linked with a [conjN] labelled arrow, where N is a conjunct's place in an ordering of all the conjuncts. Turning all of this into content for a sentence of English gets us to the solution sentence (5).

(5)
their names were Flopsy, Mopsy, Cotton-tail, and Peter

    Now consider figure 3.

Figure 3: Puzzle graph combining figures 1 and 2

This third puzzle graph brings together content from the two previous puzzle graphs with connections of material at two different graph nodes. First, there is connection from the very top node of the graph with the word and(3_10). Nodes corresponding to the main verb nodes of the two earlier graphs form the dependent nodes of arrows labelled [conjN], much as we saw with the connections that led to Flopsy, Mopsy, Cotton-tail, and Peter. We can therefore expect to reach a solution sentence that is essentially the content of (3) connected by and to the content of (5).

    But there is one further point of connection that we also need to make sense of: The arrow [genv] of figure 3 does not have a node with content their as its dependent like in figure 2, but rather takes as its dependent Rabbits(3_9), which corresponds to the Rabbits(1_9) of figure 1. We can interpret this [genv] arrow connection as having an ‘invisible’ instance of their as the arrow's dependent. That is, the word their is absent from the puzzle graph because it's presence is captured as a direct arrow connection (referential link) to Rabbits(3_9), which occurs in the puzzle graph for independent reasons. We are therefore justified to take ‘(3) and (5)’ — namely (6) — to be the overall solution sentence for the puzzle graph of figure 3.

(6)
Once upon a time there were four little Rabbits, and their names were Flopsy, Mopsy, Cotton-tail, and Peter.

3.2   An example with ‘#’

Figure 4 has a similar structure to figure 3, in the sense that there are two verbs connected by [conj1] and [conj2] arrows from and(4_10) occurring topmost in the graph. However, while the verb of the first conjunct is overtly realised with lost(4_2), the content for the verb of the second conjunct is shown as #4_1001 to indicate the licensed absence of a verb (a lost lost!) from the solution sentence. Note that the indexing with ‘#’ involves two numbers without round brackets. The first number says which solution sentence the elision belongs to. The second number follows a count of the number of elision instances that occur within the sentence, starting from 1000. This gives indexing that is distinct from the word location indexing within round brackets that accompanies each word.

Figure 4: Puzzle graph with ‘#’

Following all the links of figure 4, we can arrive at solution sentence (7).

(7)
He lost one of his shoes among the cabbages, and the other shoe amongst the potatoes.

3.3   An example with a reverse link

Figure 4 has [arg1] as the label for arrows that connect a governor node contributing verb content to a dependent node corresponding to the object for the verb. Links can be reversed by adding [of] to the arrow label, so with label [arg1][of] the arrow connects an object as the governor node to verb content as its dependent node. Such a node label occurs in Figure 5.

Figure 5: Puzzle graph with a reverse link

    Reverse link [arg1][of] in figure 5 can be interpreted as a clue to capture the graph relation with a relative clause in the solution sentence. The governor of the [arg1][of] reverse link (sieve(5_7)) functions as the relativised element with [arg1] (object) function for pop(5_12) as content internal to the relative clause. With such a perspective on the contribution of reverse links, (8) can be given as the solution sentence for the graph.

(8)
Mr. McGregor came up with a sieve , which he intended to pop upon the top of Peter

3.4   A complex puzzle

This section demonstrates how to approach a complex puzzle. We will break this into stages, first building content for simpler graphs, which then combine to give larger graphs, and so ultimately lead to the final graph for reaching the overall solution sentence.

    To start, consider figure 6.

Figure 6: Puzzle graph for a single clause

By finding [arg0], we can identify was(6_3) as the main verb, and Mr.(6_1)_McGregor(6_2) as the ‘be-er’ (subject). One other arrow extends from was(6_3) to and(6_7) and this is labelled with the preposition word on(6_4) and the functional information [clr][loc] (closely related location). In turn, and(6_7) takes us to two conjuncts via the labelled connections [conj1] leading to hands(6_6) and [conj2] leading to knees(6_8). One more connection, labelled [genv] (genitive/possessive), originates from and(6_7) and leads to Mr.(6_1)_McGregor(6_2) to capture the information that hands(6_6) and knees(6_8) are those of Mr. McGregor. By following these connections and including his to capture the genitive/possessive connection, we can reach (9) as the solution sentence.

(9)
Mr. McGregor was on his hands and knees

    Now consider figure 7.

Figure 7: Puzzle content for a participle clause

An arrow labelled [arg1] (object) extends from planting(7_1)_out(7_2) to cabbages(7_4). In turn, cabbages(7_4) leads via an [attribute] labelled arrow to young(7_3). By following these connections of the graph, we reach the sentence fragment (10).

(10)
planting out young cabbages

    Now consider figure 8.

Figure 8: Puzzle graph combining figures 6 and 7

Figure 8 has connections to bring together node content from figures 6 and 7. There is a [scon][cnt]#8_1001 (continuative subordinate conjunction) connection to state that planting(8_9)_out(8_10) (/planting(6_1)_out(6_2)) is subordinate to was(8_3) (/was(6_3)). There is also an [arg0] connection from planting(8_9)_out(8_10) (/planting(6_1)_out(6_2)) to Mr.(8_1)_McGregor(8_2) (/Mr.(6_1)_McGregor(6_2)), the ‘be-er’ (subject) of the main clause. A solution sentence that meets this new criteria of Figure 8 is (11).

(11)
Mr. McGregor was on his hands and knees planting out young cabbages

    Now consider figure 9.

Figure 9: Puzzle graph for a single clause

Looking at jumped(9_2), this is linked to he(9_1) with an [arg0] arrow, and to up(9_3) with a [clr][dir] (closely related directional) arrow. Therefore, we can identify jumped as the main verb, he as the ‘do-er’ (subject), and up as an adverbial modifier, to derive the simple sentence of (12).

(12)
he jumped up

    Now consider figure 10.

Figure 10: Puzzle graph for another single clause

Looking at ran(10_2), this is linked to he(10_1) with an [arg0] arrow. Also, ran(10_2) is linked to Peter(10_4) with an arrow that contains the preposition word after(10_3) and the function information [clr][dir] (closely related direction). With these words and connections, we can derive the solution sentence (13).

(13)
he ran after Peter

    Now consider figure 11.

Figure 11: Puzzle graph combining figures 9 and 10

This combines figures 9 and 10, with and(11_4) to connect jumped(11_2) (/jumped(9_2)) and ran(11_5) (/ran(10_2)). Note how the single he(11_1) (/he(9_1)/he(10_1)) connects as the ‘do-er’ (subject) to both verbs. A solution sentence is (14).

(14)
he jumped up and ran after Peter

    Now consider figure 12.

Figure 12: Puzzle graph for a participle clause

We can see waving(12_1) is connected to rake(12_3) with an [arg1] ‘done to’ (object) arrow. A solution sentence fragment is (15).

(15)
waving a rake

    Now consider figure 13.

Figure 13: Puzzle graph for an imperative clause

The [clausetype] arrow that extends from Stop(13_1) tells us that Stop is the verb of the clause. This connects to [imp] to tell us that the clause is an imperative. Typically, an imperative is an order or instruction for an unmentioned ‘do-er’ to do something, but also the ‘do-er’ can be addressed with a [voc] (vocative) connection. A vocative — figure 13 has thief(13_2) — can occur either at the front or end of an imperative, making (16) a possible solution sentence.

(16)
Stop thief!

    Now consider figure 14.

Figure 14: Puzzle graph for a clause with an embedding with the content of figure 13

This is the puzzle graph for a clause with an embedding with the content of figure 13. This embedding is connected by an [arg1] (object) arrow to the verb content calling(14_1) (with [clr][dir] (closely related directional) modifier out(14_2)). A solution sentence fragment is (17).

(17)
calling out, ‘Stop thief!’

    Now consider figure 15.

Figure 15: Puzzle graph combining figures 12 and 14

Figure 15 results from combining figure 12 and 14. Here, and(15_4) has conjuncts waving(15_1) (/waving(12_1)) and calling(15_5) (/calling(14_1)). A solution sentence fragment is (18).

(18)
waving a rake and calling out, ‘Stop thief!’

    Now consider figure 16.

Figure 16: Puzzle graph combining figures 11 and 15

Figure 16 results from combining figures 11 and 15, with the exception that there is now a [scon][cnt]#16_1001 (continuative subordinate conjunction) labelled coordination connection, much like we saw happen with figure 8. As a consequence of this change, waving and calling add to the information about the ‘do-er’ (subject) of the main clause. Note how the verbs jumped(16_2), ran(16_5), waving(16_8), and calling(16_12) all have [arg0] ‘do-er’ (subject) arrows that connect to the same he(16_1). A solution sentence is (19).

(19)
he jumped up and ran after Peter, waving a rake and calling out, ‘Stop thief!’

    We are finally in a position to consider the overall puzzle graph of figure 17.

Figure 17: Puzzle graph combining figures 8 and 16

This combines figures 8 and 16, with but(17_13) connecting to was(17_3) (corresponding to was(8_3) of figure 8) and and(17_17) (corresponding to and(16_4) of figure 16). Note how the verbs was(17_3), planting(17_9)_out(6_5), jumped(17_15), ran(17_18), waving(17_21), and calling(17_25) all have [arg0] ‘be-er/do-er’ (subject) arrows that connect to the same instance of Mr.(17_1)_McGregor(17_2). An overall solution sentence for figure 17 is (20).

(20)
Mr. McGregor was on his hands and knees planting out young cabbages, but he jumped up and ran after Peter, waving a rake and calling out, ‘Stop thief!’
